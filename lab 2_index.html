1. Rabbit Foot Fern Davallia sp. Scientific name: Davallia sp.
Family: Davalliaceae
Division: Pteridophyta
Class: Polypodiopsida
Order: Polypodiales

golden hairs, often seen clinging to tree trunks or rocks. Fronds are triangular to ovate, 2–4 times pinnate,
and leathery in texture. Stipes are slender and wiry with scales at the base. Sori are round or oblong, found near
frond margins, each protected by a cup-shaped indusium.

Habitat and distribution: Commonly found growing on tree trunks and rocks in shaded,humid forest
environments. Distributed throughout Southeast Asia, including Malaysia.

Diagnostic features: Recognized by its furry rhizome (“rabbit’s foot” appearance) and cup-shaped indusium,
which distinguish it from other fern genera.		 - Used for ornamental value such

4. Bog Clubmoss (Paku Serani) Lycopodiella sp. Scientific name: Lycopodiella sp.
Family: Lycopodiaceae
Division: Lycopodiophyta
Class: Lycopodiopsida
Order: Lycopodiales

Commonly found in moist, open areas such as swampy grounds, peat soils, riverbanks, and forest edges.
It prefers acidic, nutrient-poor soils and is often associated with disturbed or pioneer habitats. Distributed widely in tropical and subtropical regions, including Malaysia and other parts of Southeast Asia.

Diagnostic features:
Presence of strobili (spore-bearing cones) on upright fertile stems.
Small, scale-like microphylls arranged spirally around the stem.
Lacks true leaves, flowers, and seeds — reproduces by spores.
Stems are creeping and rooting periodically at the nodes.		 - Used in traditional medicines to treat conditions like impairment, rhematism and skin irrations.

5. Spikemosses Selaginella sp. Scientific name: Selaginella sp.
Family: Selaginellaceae
Division: Lycopodiophyta
Class: Lycopodiopsida
Order: Selaginellales

Morphological characteristics:
A small herbaceous lycophyte with creeping or ascending stems, often forming dense mats or patches. Stems are slender and branched,
bearing numerous tiny, scale-like microphylls arranged in four rows (two dorsal and two ventral).

Leaves (microphylls):
Simple, with a single unbranched vein and a ligule (small membranous outgrowth) at the leaf base.

Reproductive structures:
Bears strobili (cones) at the stem tips, composed of sporophylls that enclose sporangia.
Each strobilus produces two types of spores — large megaspores and small microspores, making the genus heterosporous.
Reproduction occurs through spores, not seeds or flowers.

Habitat and distribution:
Typically found in moist, shaded forest floors, rocky slopes, and damp, mossy areas. Some species can tolerate partial sunlight and grow along riverbanks or waterfalls.
Widely distributed throughout tropical and subtropical regions, including Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand.

Diagnostic features:
Four-ranked microphyll arrangement. Presence of ligules at the leaf bases (unique to Selaginella).
Heterosporous reproduction with distinct megaspores and microspores. Creeping habit forming carpet-like growth.		 - Used in traditional medicine for conditions like wounds and menstrual disorders as a food source and ornamental value.
6. Bird's Nest Fern Asplenium nidus Scientific name: Asplenium nidus
Family: Aspleniaceae
Division: Pteridophyta
Class: Polypodiopsida
Order: Polypodiales

Morphological characteristics:
A large epiphytic fern with a short, thick rhizome and a rosette of simple, undivided fronds that radiate outward to form a nest-like structure.

Fronds:
Broad, lanceolate to oblanceolate, leathery, and bright green, reaching up to 1–2 m in length. The midrib is prominent and dark
brown to black, extending from base to tip. Margins are entire and wavy.

Rhizome:
Short and erect, densely covered with dark brown scales.

Sori:
Arranged in parallel oblique lines on the lower surface of the fronds, each protected by a linear indusium that opens toward the midrib.

Reproduction:
Reproduces via spores produced in sori; no flowers or seeds.

Habitat and distribution:
Commonly grows as an epiphyte on tree trunks and branches in humid tropical rainforests, but can also occur as a terrestrial fern in shaded, moist areas.
Prefers high humidity and indirect light. Widely distributed throughout Southeast Asia, including Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines, and also found in tropical regions worldwide.

Diagnostic features:
Formation of a funnel-shaped rosette of large, undivided fronds (“bird’s nest” appearance). Parallel oblique sori on the underside of fronds.
Prominent black midrib and entire leaf margin.		 - Used a a popular houseplant in garden or container plant.

- traditionally used in various culture to treat conditions like asthma, sores, weakness and halitosis.

- The young fronds are edible for cooked. The largae frond also can be use for wrapping.

Commonly found in open forests, forest edges, clearings, and secondary growth areas.
It thrives in moist soils and sunny to partially shaded environments. Distributed widely throughout tropical and subtropical regions, including Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, and the Pacific Islands.

Diagnostic features:
Twining or climbing fronds that coil around other plants or supports.
Dichotomously branched fronds with fertile and sterile pinnae.
Sporangia borne along leaflet margins under a thin false indusium.

10. Vegetable Fern Diplazium sp. Scientific name: Diplazium sp.
Family: Athyriaceae
Division: Pteridophyta
Class: Polypodiopsida
Order: Polypodiales

Morphological characteristics:
A terrestrial fern with a creeping to short-erect rhizome covered in light brown scales.

Fronds:
Large, pinnate to bipinnate, often forming a loose tuft. Fronds may reach 0.5–2 m in length.

Stipe (stalk):
Slender, grooved above, and scaly at the base.

Pinnae:
Lanceolate with serrate or finely toothed margins; lower pinnae slightly reduced toward the base.

Sori:
Arranged in pairs (diplaziate pattern) along both sides of the pinna midrib, linear or oblong, each covered by a linear indusium that opens toward the midrib.

Venation:
Free to slightly anastomosing (net-like).

Habitat and distribution:
Commonly found in moist, shaded areas, such as stream banks, forest understories, and ravines. Prefers damp soils and high humidity.
Widespread throughout Southeast Asia, including Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand, and extends to tropical Africa and the Pacific Islands.

Diagnostic features:
Paired linear sori on either side of the pinna midrib (a key feature of Diplazium).
Large pinnate or bipinnate fronds with toothed leaflets.
Typically terrestrial, unlike many epiphytic ferns.		 - In traditional medicine, tehy can be use to cure many illness including diabetes, asthma and fever.

Arching pinnate fronds with numerous narrow pinnae.

Two distinct frond types: basal (sterile) and antler-like (fertile).

Class: Lycopodiopsida
Order: Lycopodiales

An epiphytic or terrestrial lycophyte with erect or pendulous stems that are densely leafy.

Cylindrical, firm, and repeatedly branched; may grow erect or hanging, depending on habitat.

Leaves (microphylls):
Small, simple, lanceolate, spirally arranged, and with a single unbranched vein; margins entire or slightly toothed.

Sporangia borne singly in the axils of fertile leaves (sporophylls) rather than in distinct strobili.

Produced in sporangia that release dust-like spores for reproduction.

Habitat and distribution:
Found in moist montane forests, often growing epiphytically on tree trunks or occasionally on rocks and forest floors in shaded, humid areas.
Common in Southeast Asia, including Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand, and extending to tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Diagnostic features:
Lack of true strobilus (sporangia borne in leaf axils instead).
Erect or pendulous leafy stems densely covered with spirally arranged microphylls.
